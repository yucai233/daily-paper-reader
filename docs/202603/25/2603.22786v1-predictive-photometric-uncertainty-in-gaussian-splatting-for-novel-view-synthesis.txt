Abstract. Recent advances in 3D Gaussian Splatting have enabled im-pressive photorealistic novel view synthesis. However, to transition from a pure rendering engine to a reliable spatial map for autonomous agents and safety-critical applications, knowing where the representation is un-certain is as important as the rendering fidelity itself. We bridge this critical gap by introducing a lightweight, plug-and-play framework for pixel-wise, view-dependent predictive uncertainty estimation. Our post-hoc method formulates uncertainty as a Bayesian-regularized linear least-squares optimization over reconstruction residuals. This architecture-agnostic approach extracts a per-primitive uncertainty channel without modifying the underlying scene representation or degrading baseline vi-sual fidelity. Crucially, we demonstrate that providing this actionable reliability signal successfully translates 3D Gaussian splatting into a trustworthy spatial map, further improving state-of-the-art performance across three critical downstream perception tasks: active view selection, pose-agnostic scene change detection, and pose-agnostic anomaly detec-tion. Code will be released upon acceptance. 

Keywords: Gaussian Splatting · Novel View Synthesis · Uncertainty Estimation 

## 1 Introduction 

Radiance fields [23, 40] have evolved from novel view synthesis (NVS) engines into foundational spatial maps for autonomous agents [11, 39, 63]. However, con-structing a radiance field from 2D images is an inherently ill-posed inverse prob-lem [55]. While recent advancements have drastically improved 3DGS visual fidelity [25,61], geometric consistency [9,24], and efficiency [16,38], equipping these models with robust, system-level uncertainty estimation (UE) remains a critical, underexplored challenge. Recent efforts toward UE within 3DGS [1,21,33,52] primarily rely on stochas-tic formulations that necessitate sampling-based optimization or complex infer-ence procedures. In a systems context, these approaches introduce prohibitive latency, require fundamental architectural modifications that degrade baseline visual fidelity, and lack modularity with the rapidly expanding ecosystem of 3DGS variants. Conversely, existing post-hoc alternatives focus primarily on modeling epistemic uncertainty within the parameter space of 3DGS via Hes-sian approximations [21, 58]. However, as shown in our experiments (Sec. 4.1), these parameter-centric methods perform poorly when capturing view-dependent uncertainty for NVS, hindering their usage in downstream perception tasks. In this paper, we introduce an efficient, plug-and-play system for pixel-wise predictive uncertainty estimation in 3DGS novel view synthesis. Our approach generates view-dependent uncertainty maps alongside RGB images during novel view synthesis, with areas of high uncertainty reflecting regions of low rendering fidelity in the novel view (see Fig. 1). We demonstrate the practical utility of this approach across three downstream perception tasks, leveraging our uncertainty estimates to improve performance for next-best-view planning [43], pose-agnostic scene change detection [13], and pose-agnostic anomaly detection [31]. Yet reconstruction resid-uals alone are not a reliable proxy for uncertainty – in sparse-view settings, the highly parameterized nature of 3DGS enables the model to overfit to the training views, driving residuals toward zero (thus signaling zero predictive uncertainty). To mitigate this, we introduce a Bayesian prior within our linear least-squares formulation of uncertainty estimation. This prior encourages uncertainty esti-mates to revert toward a maximal uncertainty level when insufficient multi-view evidence is available. This process is entirely post-hoc: it requires zero modifica-tions to the base architecture or its optimization pipeline, ensuring preservation of the original rendering fidelity and compatibility with any 3DGS variant. In summary, we make the following claims: 

– We introduce a lightweight, plug-and-play system for view-dependent UE for novel view synthesis in 3DGS. We achieve state-of-the-art performance for UE while strictly preserving rendering fidelity (see Sec. 4.1). 

– We formulate this estimation as a (Bayesian-inspired) regularized linear least-squares problem, which ensures robust uncertainty predictions across both dense and sparse capture settings (see Sec. 4.2). 

– Beyond state-of-the-art UE in 3DGS, our uncertainty maps can be practi-cally utilized in downstream tasks to improve state-of-the-art performance in active view selection (Sec. 4.3), pose-agnostic scene change detection (Sec. 4.4), and pose-agnostic anomaly detection (Sec. 4.5). 

## 2 Related Work 

We review prior work on UE in Gaussian Splatting (Sec. 2.1) and the three downstream tasks, active view selection (Sec. 2.2), pose-agnostic scene change detection (Sec. 2.3), and pose-agnostic anomaly detection (Sec. 2.4). 

2.1 Uncertainty Estimation in Gaussian Splatting 

UE in NeRFs [40] has been extensively explored through stochastic sampling [50], ensemble models [54], conditional normalizing flows [51], and provenance model-ing [41]. Bayes’ Rays [14] interprets uncertainty as allowable volumetric variation to identify under-constrained regions. While foundational, these designs remain tightly coupled to neural volumetric representations, making direct application to 3DGS [23] non-trivial due to its explicit, non-neural parameterization. Existing UE techniques for 3DGS primarily rely on stochastic formulations. Continuous Semantic Splatting [59] learns semantic distributions over Gaus-sian primitives, interpreting their variance as a measure of semantic uncertainty. Several stochastic extensions [1,33,37] derive pixel-level uncertainty from multi-sample variance. Stochastic-GS [1] learns probabilistic distributions over Gaus-sian parameters, while Variational-3DGS [33] leverages multi-scale Gaussian hi-erarchies to construct a diversified sampling space. Lyu et al . [37] propose a 4 Galappaththige et al. 

low-dimensional manifold constraint to reduce the heavy sampling cost inher-ent in stochastic rendering. Despite their advances, these stochastic methods are computationally expensive and demand substantial architectural or optimization modifications. This lacks plug-and-play applicability across 3DGS variants and often compromises rendering fidelity. Current post-hoc approaches for uncertainty estimation in 3DGS predomi-nantly focus on the parameter space of the model. FisherRF [21] uses Fisher information to quantify confidence in the learned representation. POp-GS [58] extends this via optimal experimental design while also modeling inter-parameter correlations. Similar to PRIMU [15], our approach operates directly in image space. However, rather than relying on hand-crafted features and a regressor trained on hold-out views, we introduce a lightweight linear least-squares for-mulation. This allows us to directly learn primitive-level, view-dependent uncer-tainty that efficiently aggregates into pixel-wise maps from arbitrary viewpoints, entirely bypassing the need for a regressor. A parallel line of work assigns error-based scores to primitives to guide prun-ing and densification. PUP 3D-GS [17] and Speedy-Splat [16] compute pruning scores based on L2 loss sensitivity. Rota Bulò et al . [44] derive a scalar den-sification score directly from the rendering error. However, their per-pixel for-mulation ignores interactions between overlapping Gaussians, assigning elevated scalar scores to all contributing primitives even if only one is responsible for an artifact. While this heuristic suits densification, where over-densification is corrected in subsequent optimization, it falsely penalizes reliable primitives in frozen models. By analyzing all views simultaneously through our least-squares formulation, we disentangle these interactions to consistently identify correct Gaussians, even when they contribute to localized artifacts. 

2.2 Active View Selection in Gaussian Splatting 

Active view selection (AVS) seeks the optimal next viewpoint to maximize re-construction quality under a constrained acquisition budget [21, 29, 37, 43, 54]. Standard approaches select a candidate view from a predefined pool to either minimize the overall uncertainty [37, 54] or maximize the expected information gain [21,43]. Within 3DGS specifically, FisherRF [21] optimizes information gain between candidate and training views, whereas Lyu et al . [37] reduce reconstruc-tion uncertainty via low-dimensional manifold sampling. In contrast, we adopt a deterministic strategy that selects the next view to maximally reduce the per-primitive uncertainty estimated from our least-squares residual formulation, achieving state-of-the-art results in AVS. 

2.3 Pose-Agnostic Scene Change Detection 

Scene change detection (SCD) has been widely studied as a bi-temporal com-parison problem, where changes are identified between two aligned images [2, 7, 10,32,34,46–48,56]. Such methods rely on strict viewpoint alignment and exten-sive supervision, limiting scalability in real-world applications such as robotic 3DGS-U 5

inspection [13]. Recent studies [3, 8, 22, 26] explore zero-shot SCD using large vi-sual foundation models [27, 42]; however, they still assume paired captures with minimal viewpoint disparity. The emergence of high-fidelity 3D scene representations [23, 60] has enabled 

pose-agnostic SCD, where pre-change scenes are reconstructed and rendered into post-change viewpoints for comparison [12, 13, 20, 36]. However, they re-main highly sensitive to reconstruction inaccuracies and view-dependent arti-facts, which are frequently misclassified as true scene changes. We address this critical flaw by leveraging our predicted per-pixel uncertainty to suppress unre-liable regions, effectively filtering out rendering-induced false positives. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to apply uncertainty estimation to pose-agnostic SCD. We further demonstrate that incorporating our uncer-tainty maps improves state-of-the-art pose-agnostic SCD methods [13], as our UE accurately captures erroneous regions in novel-view renderings. 

2.4 Pose-Agnostic Anomaly Detection 

Traditional visual anomaly detection (AD) methods [5,45] excel at localizing de-fects but typically require strictly controlled, fixed camera viewpoints. To relax this constraint, recent approaches leverage 3D scene representations [23, 40] to enable pose-agnostic AD [31,35,62], allowing query captures from arbitrary view-points to be directly compared against novel-view renderings of a reconstructed reference model of an object. However, much like in pose-agnostic SCD, this di-rect comparison introduces a critical vulnerability: systemic rendering artifacts and geometric ambiguities are frequently misclassified as physical defects. By explicitly quantifying the reliability of the reference model, our view-dependent uncertainty maps provide a principled mechanism to mask these inherently am-biguous regions. This effectively suppresses rendering-induced false positives, significantly enhancing the robustness of the AD pipeline. 

## 3 Method

Subsequently, we describe our learning formulation (Sec. 3.3) and introduce a Bayesian-inspired regularization (Sec. 3.4) that improves uncertainty consistency in highly novel viewpoints.

3.4 Bayesian Inspired Regularization 

The least-squares formulation in Eq. (4) learns directional uncertainty only from viewing directions that are present in the training data. If a primitive is never observed from a certain direction, the optimization receives no signal about how uncertain it should be in that direction. Furthermore, in sparse-view scenarios, a trivially low rendering error on the training views often masks a poorly recon-structed underlying 3D geometry. As a result, uncertainty estimates for highly novel viewpoints can become unstable or unintuitive. To address this, we introduce an L2 regularization that acts as a prior over the directional uncertainty functions. Intuitively, an unobserved primitive should default to a state of maximal uncertainty. We therefore encourage the learned uncertainty, uk(d), to default toward a maximal uncertainty level b ∈ R in di-rections unsupported by training views, while still allowing observed directions to adapt to the reconstruction residuals. This approach is mathematically grounded in the well-known interpretation of L2-regularized linear regression as Bayesian inference with a Gaussian prior [6], where our prior is centered at the maximal uncertainty value b.For a Gaussian Gk, the SH coefficients of its uncertainty feature define a function uk : S2 → R on the unit sphere that encodes directional uncertainty. ## 4 Experiments 

Datasets: We follow the exact evaluation protocol established in 3DGS [23] and assess our UE performance on three standard benchmarks: Mip-NeRF360 [4], Tanks & Temples [28], and Deep Blending [18]. These datasets collectively cover a diverse range of indoor and outdoor scenes with varying levels of geomet-ric and photometric complexity, and different capturing styles [23], providing a comprehensive evaluation of uncertainty estimation in NVS. 

Baselines: We evaluate against representative state-of-the-art 3DGS UE ap-proaches, including the post-hoc method FisherRF [21] and the variational in-ference-based methods of Lyu et al. [37] (Manifold) and Li et al. [33] (Var3DGS). The latter two modify the 3DGS framework by learning distributions over Gaus-sian parameters, which slightly reduces rendering fidelity. A detailed comparison of their rendering quality against standard 3DGS is provided in the Appendix. 

Metrics: To evaluate how well predicted uncertainty maps correspond to actual reconstruction errors in NVS, we follow prior work [21, 33, 37] and report two standard metrics: Area Under the Sparsification Error curve (AUSE) [19] and Pearson correlation. AUSE measures the area between two curves obtained by progressively removing pixels in descending order of (i) true error (Oracle) and (ii) predicted uncertainty. Lower AUSE indicates stronger alignment between uncertainty and true error. Pearson correlation captures the linear relationship between per-pixel uncertainty and the error in NVS. Higher correlation reflects better alignment of predicted uncertainty and actual error. We report both metrics using two error formulations: the standard intensity difference L1, and DSSIM. DSSIM is particularly important for evaluating errors in NVS, as many rendering errors in NVS arise from structural artifacts such as geometric misalignment, blur, or floaters rather than simple pixel-wise intensity deviations. Since DSSIM is sensitive to luminance, contrast, and structural in-consistencies, it provides a more perceptually meaningful measure of NVS error than L1, which often underestimates geometry- or blur-related discrepancies.

4.2 UE on Highly Novel Views Experiment Setup: We investigate the contribution of our Bayesian-inspired regularization on highly novel views by adopting a sparse setup using four well-distributed training views. These four views are selected by maximizing pairwise camera center distances. We retain the test views in the standard setting (as in Sec. 4.1 and 3DGS [23]) for fair comparison. The train-test split is visualized in 3DGS-U 11 

Fig. 5a. Due to the sparse coverage of the scene, most test views observe the scene from highly novel viewing directions. We evaluate on Mip-NeRF360 [4], training the base 3DGS model for 4,000 it-erations and our uncertainty channel for 400 iterations to account for the sparse view count. For experiments with Bayesian-inspired regularization, we set the maximal uncertainty level to b = 1 . This choice follows naturally from our train-ing objective, as uncertainty is regressed toward the photometric reconstruction residual, whose magnitude is bounded by the normalized image intensity range. Thus, a value of 1 corresponds to maximal reconstruction error and provides a principled prior level. In these regularized experiments, background regions are likewise treated as maximally uncertain, assigning an uncertainty value of 1 to regions not covered by any primitives. No explicit background prior is imposed when Bayesian regularization is disabled. As a baseline, we use FisherRF [21], since it is the only publicly available post-hoc approach that can be directly applied to the same splatting outputs as our method. 

Analysis of Results: In Fig. 5b, we perform a parameter study on the regu-larization weight λreg to analyze the effect of our proposed regularization. Our approach con-sistently outperforms FisherRF across all λreg values in this challenging setting. Compared to the standard UE study, AUSE scores are generally higher, likely due to reduced splatting quality and fewer UE training views, which can make the residual error maps inconsistent and harder to learn. Overall, the Bayesian-inspired regularization improves performance in this regime, though its effect differs between error metrics. ## 5 Conclusion 

We introduced a lightweight, plug-and-play framework for view-dependent, pre-dictive photometric uncertainty estimation for NVS in 3DGS. By formulating post-hoc uncertainty estimation as a linear least-squares problem with Bayesian-inspired regularization, our method allows for effective prediction of pixel-level uncertainty maps under both dense and sparse captures. This enables new state-of-the-art results in active view selection, pose-agnostic scene change detection, and anomaly detection. ## F Limitations 

A fundamental limitation of our approach arises from its strict post-hoc nature. Since we learn uncertainty at the primitive level and render it via standard splat-ting, the spatial granularity of our uncertainty maps is intrinsically bottlenecked by the density and scale of the underlying 3D Gaussians. Consequently, in re-gions where the scene geometry is only coarsely represented (e.g., by a few large primitives), our predicted uncertainty will inevitably exhibit a similarly coarse resolution. While this could be mitigated by allowing the optimization process to dynamically spawn, split, or reshape Gaussians in highly uncertain regions, we explicitly opted against altering the underlying geometry. Our core objective is to provide a lightweight, plug-and-play uncertainty estimator for existing rep-resentations, ensuring that the original rendering fidelity and spatial structure of the frozen 3DGS model remain completely preserved.
