Wen Cao 1 and Daniel Jönsson 2 and Jonas Unger 3

Abstract 

Accurate BRDF acquisition is important for realistic rendering, but dense gonioreflectometer measurements are slow and expensive. We study how to select a small number of BRDF measurements that are most useful for reconstructing material appearance under a learned reflectance prior. Our method combines a set encoder for sparse coordinate-value observations, a pretrained hypernetwork-based BRDF reconstructor, and a differentiable renderer. During sampler training, the reconstructor is kept fixed and gradients from BRDF-space and rendered-image losses are used to optimize measurement locations. This sepa-rates sample selection from prior fitting and encourages the sampler to choose directions that are informative under the learned material distribution. Experiments on the MERL dataset show that the proposed sampler improves low-budget reconstruction quality at 8 and 16 measurements compared with neural reconstruction baselines, while PCA-based methods remain strong at larger budgets. We further analyze the effect of image-space supervision, co-optimization, and image-only latent fitting for unseen materials.

1. Introduction 

A material’s bidirectional reflectance distribution function (BRDF) determines how incident light is scattered toward a viewer and is therefore a central quantity in physically based rendering. Dense BRDF acquisition, however, is costly: conventional gonioreflec-tometer measurements require many controlled light-view config-urations, especially for glossy and specular materials whose re-flectance changes rapidly over direction. This cost limits the prac-tical use of measured materials in rendering pipelines and moti-vates acquisition strategies that can recover high-quality appear-ance from far fewer samples. Inverse rendering provides a complementary source of supervi-sion. In forward rendering, a known camera, geometry, illumina-tion, and material model produce an image. In inverse rendering, the goal is to recover unknown scene or material parameters from image observations. Recent neural representations are well suited to this setting because they can act as compact priors over complex reflectance functions [Cyb89]. Rather than reconstructing a BRDF only after a fixed sampling pattern has been chosen, we ask which BRDF measurements should be acquired in the first place so that a learned reconstructor can reproduce the observed appearance. We propose an end-to-end framework that learns sparse BRDF sampling patterns from image-domain supervision. Sparse coordinate–value measurements are encoded by a set encoder, de-coded by a pretrained hypernetwork-based BRDF reconstructor, and evaluated through differentiable rendering. Gradients from the rendered image loss are propagated back to the sampling module, encouraging it to select directions that are informative for appear-ance reconstruction under the learned BRDF prior. This design is especially useful in low-sample acquisition regimes, where every measured direction has a large effect on the final reconstruction. 

Contributions. This paper makes the following contributions: 

• a differentiable formulation for optimizing sparse BRDF mea-surement locations under a pretrained neural BRDF prior; 

• a sampler-training strategy that keeps the BRDF reconstructor fixed, reducing the risk that reconstruction capacity hides poor sample choices; 

• an evaluation of BRDF-space, image-space, and combined su-pervision across low sampling budgets on MERL materials; 

• a comparison against random, uniform, PCA-based, Hyper-BRDF, and meta-learning baselines under a shared rendering evaluation protocol; 

• an analysis of learned sample distributions, failure cases, and image-only latent fitting for unseen materials.

We review prior work in BRDF representation, efficient measured-material acquisition, and image-based material capture.

Measured BRDFs provide higher fidelity by tabulating reflectance over incident and outgo-ing directions [Rus98, Mat03], but this representation is expen-sive to acquire and store. BRDF measurement is commonly performed with goniore-flectometers, which acquire reflectance by controlling light-source and camera directions [F ∗97]. Acquiring the MERL dataset, for example, requires approximately 1.46 million directional samples per material [Rus98, Mat03]. This cost has motivated acquisition methods that reduce the number of required measurements while preserving appearance fidelity. Nielsen et al. [NJR15] and Miandji et al. [TUK17] proposed a parameterization that accelerates measurement of isotropic BRDFs within a two-dimensional slice. Dupuy and Jakob [DJ18] adaptively sampled normal distribution functions for efficient material acquisition and rendering. Liu et al. [LFR23] used meta-learning to adapt sam-ple budgets across different BRDF models. In contrast to methods driven primarily by BRDF-space reconstruction, we optimize sam-pling through an image-space rendering objective. 

Image-based neural material capture. Image-based material capture seeks to infer reflectance properties directly from pho-tographs or rendered observations [KHM ∗24, GLD ∗19]. These methods typically train neural networks to predict spatially vary-ing material parameters such as normal, diffuse albedo, rough-ness, metallicity, or specular response. Deschaintre et al. [DAD ∗19] used an encoder–decoder network to estimate normal, diffuse albedo, and roughness maps from phone-captured images. Zhang et al. [ZSD ∗21] inferred per-point reflectance from images using a NeRF-based formulation. Recent diffusion-based methods decom-pose RGB images into normal, albedo, roughness, metallicity, and diffuse components [Zen24, LCY ∗24]. Our goal is different: we use images not only to infer material appearance, but also to learn which sparse BRDF measurements are most useful for later acqui-sition. 

3. Method 

We learn sparse BRDF measurement locations by coupling a pre-trained BRDF reconstruction prior with a differentiable rendering loss. The central question is not how to reconstruct a BRDF from an already chosen sampling pattern, but which small set of incident– outgoing directions should be measured so that the reconstructed BRDF reproduces the target appearance.

Classic subspace methods such as PCA have shown that carefully chosen BRDF samples can be highly informative for measured-material reconstruction [NJR15]. Our sampler plays an analogous role, but the sample locations are optimized through a neural BRDF prior and a rendering-aware objective instead of being selected only for linear BRDF-space reconstruction. To enable end-to-end optimization, the sampling process must be differentiable with respect to the sample locations, follow-ing [LFR23]. 3.5. Training Protocols 

Training proceeds in three stages. First, we use a pretrained BRDF reconstructor Gη, learned from dense measured BRDFs, as the ma-terial prior. Second, for each sampling budget N, we freeze Gη

and optimize the sampler parameters θ, together with the set en-coder when applicable, using Equation 8. Freezing the reconstruc-tor forces the optimization to improve the measurement locations rather than hiding poor samples inside additional reconstruction ca-pacity. Third, we optionally refine the latent representation or en-coder jointly with the sampler while still keeping the reconstructor fixed; this is the co-optimization setting evaluated in the ablation study. At inference time, the learned sampling pattern SN 

> θ

is used to acquire N BRDF measurements for the material. These sparse ob-servations are passed through the set encoder and the fixed recon-structor to produce ˆ fm, which can then be rendered under arbitrary test views and lighting. • a set encoder that aggregates sparse BRDF coordinate–value ob-servations into a compact latent code z;

• a hypernetwork-based reconstructor that decodes z to produce a full BRDF representation (or the parameters of a neural BRDF model); 

• a differentiable renderer that produces images under fixed scene and illumination conditions. 

4. Implementation Details 

We implement the differentiable rendering pipeline using Mit-suba 3 [JSR ∗22] and multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) in Py-Torch [PGM ∗19]. The neural components are embedded in the ren-dering loop so that gradients can propagate between the BRDF re-constructor, the image formation process, and the sampling mod-ule. Measured reflectance values often have high dynamic range, par-ticularly near specular peaks. We therefore preprocess BRDF val-ues using Log-Relative Mapping (LRM) [NJR15], which reduces the dynamic range and improves numerical stability during opti-mization. 

4.1. Neural Pipeline 

The neural pipeline maps sparse BRDF observations to a compact latent representation. The set encoder receives sampled BRDF val-ues together with their corresponding directional coordinates and outputs a 40-dimensional embedding. Conditioned on this embed-ding, a hypernetwork-based decoder reconstructs a dense BRDF over the Rusinkiewicz parameterization [GSZ ∗24], which can then be evaluated by the differentiable renderer. 

4.2. Training 

The implementation follows the training protocol in Equation 8. For each sampling budget, we initialize a sampling pattern in the valid BRDF domain, query the corresponding preprocessed BRDF values, and pass the resulting coordinate–value set through the en-coder and frozen reconstructor. The reconstructed BRDF is then converted to the renderable representation used by Mitsuba 3. Gra-dients from the BRDF prediction loss and the rendered-image loss are accumulated on the sampler parameters and, in the co-optimization setting, on the encoder or latent representation. The reconstructor remains fixed throughout sampler training. 

5. Experiments 5.1. Datasets 

To evaluate the effectiveness of our method, we conduct experiments on the widely used MERL measured BRDF dataset [MPBM03]. The dataset contains real-world isotropic re-flectance measurements and has been extensively adopted in prior work on BRDF acquisition and learning-based reconstruc-tion [GSZ ∗24, NJR15, LFR23]. The MERL dataset comprises 100 measured materials spanning a broad range of appearances, including diffuse, glossy, and highly specular behaviors. Each material is provided as densely sampled reflectance values over the incident–outgoing directional domain under the Rusinkiewicz parameterization [Rus98]. For evaluation and comparison with prior methods in Table 2, we use a fixed train/test split: 90 materials for training and 10 held-out materials for testing. All methods reported in Table 2 are evaluated using the same split and dataset settings.

5.3.1. Optimization Regimes 

We first evaluate the effect of different optimization regimes while keeping the evaluation protocol fixed. In the sampling-only regime, only the sampling pattern is optimized, while the BRDF latent rep-resentation ZBRDF and the reconstruction network are kept fixed. This setting isolates the contribution of the learned sample loca-tions. Since the sampler is optimized from scratch, this stage typi-cally requires approximately 1000 epochs to converge. We then consider a co-optimization regime. Starting from the sampling pattern learned in the sampling-only stage, we jointly re-fine the sampler and the BRDF latent representation. In this setting, both the sampling pattern and ZBRDF are optimized using the same reconstruction objective. Thanks to the strong initialization pro-vided by the sampling-only stage, the joint refinement converges rapidly and typically requires only about 50 epochs. As shown in the co-optimization columns of Table 1, joint refine-ment consistently improves PSNR over the sampling-only variant. The improvement is approximately 1 dB for several tested sampling budgets, indicating that adapting the latent representation together with the sample locations can improve rendered appearance. 

5.3.2. Supervision Objectives 

We next compare two supervision objectives within the sampling-only training setting. In the BRDF-space supervision setting, the predicted BRDF values are directly compared against reference BRDF measurements. The training objective minimizes this BRDF reconstruction error, encouraging accurate recovery of the re-flectance function at the sampled directions. In the image-space supervision setting, supervision is applied after rendering. Given the predicted BRDF, we render images under the same illumina-tion and viewing conditions as the reference images, and minimize the reconstruction error between the rendered outputs and the ref-erences. This objective directly optimizes the final appearance and naturally accounts for how BRDF errors affect rendered images. Comparing the first two columns of Table 1, we observe that nei-ther supervision objective uniformly dominates the other. Image-space supervision performs better for some sampling budgets, while BRDF-space supervision is preferable in others. This sug-gests that the two objectives emphasize different aspects of the reconstruction problem: BRDF-space supervision improves point-wise reflectance accuracy, whereas image-space supervision better aligns the optimization with the final rendered appearance. 

5.4. Comparison with Prior Methods 

We compare our method with three prior approaches: Hyper-BRDF [GSZ ∗24], a PCA-based reconstruction baseline [NJR15], and a meta-learning method [LFR23]. We evaluate all methods us-ing peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) and structural similarity in-dex (SSIM). Both metrics are computed on rendered images and averaged over the held-out test set. 5.5. Sample-location analysis 

Figure 1 illustrates the visual quality of BRDFs reconstructed from our learned sparse sample selections. We render a scene containing multiple materials and visualize the corresponding reconstruction results. For each material, we also show the selected representa-tive sampling locations, which provide insight into how the learned sampler adapts to different reflectance properties. As shown in Figure 4, the number of samples required for high-fidelity reconstruction varies across material types. For fabric and diffuse materials, approximately 8–16 samples are sufficient to achieve visually accurate results. In contrast, specular and pheno-lic materials require substantially more samples, often up to 128, to capture their sharper and more complex reflectance behavior. These results suggest that the learned sampling strategy allocates more sampling capacity to materials with higher angular complex-ity, while simpler BRDFs can be reconstructed reliably from only a small number of observations. 

5.6. New-material Validation 

To further evaluate the practical applicability of the proposed ap-proach, we consider previously unseen materials for which dense measured BRDF data are not available. Unlike the standard evalua-tion setting, where reference BRDF measurements can be used for supervision, this scenario only provides image observations cap-tured under controlled illumination and viewing conditions. We therefore assess reconstruction quality in the rendered image do-main. For this experiment, we keep the pretrained hypernetwork fixed and omit the set encoder at inference time. Rather than estimating the latent code from explicit BRDF samples, we directly optimize the latent variable z for each material by minimizing an image-space reconstruction loss. Specifically, we render images from the BRDF predicted by the hypernetwork and update z to minimize the discrepancy between these renderings and the reference im-ages. The resulting optimized latent code, denoted by z⋆, provides an analysis-by-synthesis estimate of the material appearance. We then use z⋆ as a teacher representation to guide the sparse-sampling stage, following the same optimization procedure used in our method. This validation demonstrates that the approach can be applied when explicit BRDF measurements are unavailable: a latent material representation is first fitted from images and then used to derive sparse sample selections. 6. Limitations 

Our current evaluation has several limitations. First, the learned sampler is trained and evaluated on the isotropic MERL dataset, so the results do not yet establish performance for anisotropic, spa-tially varying, translucent, or layered materials. Second, the image-space objective depends on the chosen scene, lighting, camera, and tone mapping; sample locations optimized for one rendering setup may not be optimal for another. Third, our experiments use measured BRDF tables and differentiable interpolation rather than a physical acquisition device. A hardware-in-the-loop implemen-tation would require calibration, motor constraints, measurement noise modeling, and quantization of optimized directions. Finally, PCA-based methods remain stronger at larger sampling budgets, suggesting that future work should combine rendering-aware losses with stronger BRDF-space priors. 

7. Conclusion 

We presented a differentiable framework for learning sparse BRDF measurement locations from image supervision. By combining a learned BRDF prior, a set encoder for sparse observations, and dif-ferentiable rendering, the proposed method optimizes samples ac-cording to their effect on final rendered appearance. The results show that the proposed sampler is most useful in the extremely sparse regime. At 8 and 16 samples, our method achieves the high-est PSNR among the compared neural baselines and improves over non-adaptive sample choices. At larger budgets, PCA-based re-construction benefits from denser direct observations and achieves higher PSNR and SSIM. We therefore view the proposed method as complementary to PCA-style acquisition: it is advantageous when only a very small number of measurements is affordable, while classical subspace methods remain highly competitive when more samples are available.
