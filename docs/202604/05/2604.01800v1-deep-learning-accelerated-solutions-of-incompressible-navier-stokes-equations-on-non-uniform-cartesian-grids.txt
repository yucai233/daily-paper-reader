## Deep learning accelerated solutions of incompressible Navier-Stokes equations on non-uniform Cartesian grids 

Heming Bai a, Dong Zhang b, Shengze Cai c, Xin Bian a, ∗

Abstract 

The pressure Poisson equation (PPE) represents the primary computational bottleneck in fractional step methods for incompressible flow simulations, requiring iterative solutions of large-scale linear systems. We previously introduced HyDEA (Hybrid Deep lEarning line-search directions and iterative methods for Accelerated solutions, Journal of Com-putational Physics, 554, 2026, 114747), a hybrid approach that synergizes deep neural networks with classical iterative solvers to accelerate the PPE solution process. However, its current implementation is limited to uniform Cartesian grids. Accurately resolving complex flow dynamics near solid boundaries requires local grid refinement, yet extending the original HyDEA to non-uniform Cartesian grids is fundamentally challenging, as its standard convolution operators are inherently ill-suited for processing data with spatially varying resolutions. To address this limitation, we adopt the Mesh-Conv (MConv) operator (Journal of Computational Physics 452, 2022, 110896), which explicitly incorporates grid spacing information into convolution operations. Specifically, MConv operator replaces a subset of the standard convo-lution operators within the U-Net-based branch network of the deep operator network, with the necessary grid spacing information computed via a novel multi-level distance vector map construction strategy. Building upon this enhanced architecture, the framework seamlessly extends to simulate flows interacting with solid structures using a decoupled im-mersed boundary projection method. Furthermore, by training exclusively on fabricated linear systems rather than con-ventional flow-dependent datasets, the model generalizes e ff ortlessly across diverse immersed obstacle geometries with 

fixed neural network weights . Benchmark results demonstrate that the MConv-based HyDEA significantly outperforms both standalone preconditioned conjugate gradient methods and the standard convolution-based HyDEA in convergence performance on strongly non-uniform Cartesian grids. The robustness and exceptional generalizability of the MConv-based HyDEA underscore its potential for real-world computational fluid dynamics applications. 

Keywords: Incompressible flows, Pressure Poisson equation, Immersed boundary method, Non-uniform Cartesian grid, Deep learning line-search method, Hybrid method 

1. Introduction 

Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) has become an indispensable tool for both fundamental fluid dynamics research and engineering applications, primarily due to its ability to provide detailed flow field information via numerical solu-tions of the Navier-Stokes (NS) equations [1]. Nevertheless, CFD continues to face severe computational bottlenecks in iterative engineering applications, such as shape design optimization [2] and active flow control [3]. These applications typically require not only numerous simulations but also non-uniform spatial discretizations to accurately resolve multi-scale flow features, leading to prohibitively high computational costs. This intrinsic limitation underscores an urgent need for innovative methodologies to accelerate classical numerical solutions. The recent surge of artificial intelligence (AI) has sparked a paradigm shift within computational science [4]. Within the realm of AI-driven CFD, existing research e ff orts have largely converged into two prominent directions: physics-informed neural networks [5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13] and purely data-driven surrogate models. The latter category encompasses a diverse range of approaches, including standard neural networks-based methods [14, 15, 16], neural oper-ator learning frameworks [17, 18, 19, 20, 21, 22], and pre-training and fine-tuning strategies [23, 24, 25, 26]. Despite their theoretical appeal and rapid development, both types of frameworks frequently encounter bottlenecks regarding computa-tional accuracy, e ffi ciency, and generalizability, and thus currently remain incapable of completely supplanting traditional CFD solvers. To bridge this gap, hybrid methods have emerged as a highly promising new direction. As a pragmatic alternative, rather than replacing traditional solvers entirely, this approach preserves the well-established advantages of CFD while strategically integrating deep learning techniques to enhance specific computational components.

Within the research front of hybrid methods, a representative example is the application of neural networks to ac-celerate solutions of the pressure Poisson equation (PPE) [27, 28, 29, 30] in fractional-step methods [31, 32] for incom-pressible flows. While these early approaches have shown success, they typically rely on flow-specific training datasets, which severely limits their generalization to out-of-distribution flow scenarios. To circumvent the specific-data depen-dency, the deep conjugate direction method [33] is trained on datasets generated directly from the coe ffi cient matrix of the PPE. However, this approach relies exclusively on neural networks to update its solutions and thus inevitably suf-fers from spectral bias [34, 35, 36]. More specifically, neural networks tend to be highly e ff ective in rapidly reducing global, low-frequency errors, which is in sharp contrast to classical iterative methods that are generally better suited for attenuating local, high-frequency error components. Recognizing the inherent complementarity between neural networks and classical iterative methods has motivated a new horizon in the development of hybrid methods. By strategically har-nessing their respective strengths, recent studies have successfully accelerated numerical convergence in solving partial di ff erential equations (PDEs) [37, 38, 39, 40, 41, 42, 43]. Along this promising research line, our previous work in-troduced HyDEA (Hybrid Deep lEarning line-search directions and iterative methods for Accelerated solutions) [44] to accelerate PPE solutions within the fractional step method for incompressible flows. Specifically, HyDEA leverages a deep learning line-search method (DLSM) to rapidly eliminate low-frequency errors, while alternating it with the conju-gate gradient (CG)-type methods, which serve as a critical complement by refining the remaining solution components. Despite the success, its current implementation remains confined to flow scenarios discretized on uniform Cartesian grids, as the core architecture is based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs). In engineering applications, non-uniform grids are often desired for balancing between computational accuracy and e ffi ciency. In fluid–structure interaction (FSI) problems, for example, local grid refinement near solid boundaries is essential to resolve fine-scale flow features. Con-sequently, extending HyDEA to accommodate non-uniform grid discretizations is of significant practical and scientific value. Standard convolution operators rely on spatially shared kernel parameters, implicitly assuming that data is on uniform grids. Consequently, when processing flow fields defined on non-uniform grids, a prevalent workaround is to interpolate the data onto a uniform grid before employing convolution operators to learn the fluid dynamics [15, 22, 45]. In the presence of inner geometric obstacles, this interpolation inevitably distorts critical flow features around the boundaries. To circumvent this local distortion, a natural alternative is to employ network architectures tailored for non-uniformly distributed data. By conceptualizing grid points and their connectivity as nodes and edges, graph neural networks (GNNs) emerge as an intuitive candidate. Nevertheless, extracting the multi-scale features inherent to fluid flows presents a pro-found challenge, as classical GNNs (e.g., Graph convolutional neural network [46] and GraphSAGE [47]) su ff er from severe over-smoothing and high computational overhead, preventing the straightforward layer-stacking strategy typically used in CNNs to expand the receptive field. While advanced multi-scale GNNs, such as the architecture proposed by Lino et al. [48], achieve remarkable predictive accuracy in resolving complex flows, their U-Net-like hierarchical struc-ture incurs prohibitive computational complexity, largely dominated by the dynamic construction of graph levels and the exhaustive intra- and inter-level message-passing operations required to update node and edge features. Furthermore, treating discretized grid points as an ordered sequence of tokens enables the application of Transformer architectures [49] to model flow evolution [25]. Despite their exceptional feature extraction capabilities, the substantial computational cost of the self-attention mechanisms renders them computationally intractable for large-scale, high-resolution flow sim-ulations. Given the steep computational expenses inherent to both GNNs and Transformers when processing massive non-uniform datasets, adapting the highly e ffi cient CNN paradigm to accommodate non-uniform discretizations remains a highly compelling research frontier. To this end, Hu et al. [50] proposed the Mesh-Conv operator, which elegantly inte-grates local spatial metrics of grid spacing and angular information directly into the convolution operation. This approach significantly enhances the feature extraction on non-uniform structured grids while strictly preserving the computational effi ciency of standard convolutions. In this work, we adapt the Mesh-Conv (MConv) operator [50] to HyDEA and extend its applicability to incompress-ible flow simulations non-uniform Cartesian grids. Specifically, MConv aims to replace a subset of standard convolution operators within the U-Net based branch network of the deep operator network (DeepONet) in HyDEA. However, the original MConv operator [50] is strictly restricted to feature maps with constant spatial dimensions, rendering it incom-patible with the hierarchical downsampling and upsampling processes intrinsic to U-Net architectures. To bridge this fundamental gap, we propose a multi-level distance vector map construction strategy, which supplies the requisite grid spacing information for the MConv operators across various hierarchical levels of the U-Net. MConv-based HyDEA preserves the following key advantages: 

• Reduces the number of iterations required for PPE solution across a wide range of flow scenarios on non-uniform Cartesian grids, compared with standalone CG-type methods. 

• Achieves superior iterative convergence for solving PPE on non-uniform Cartesian grids with substantial variations in grid spacing, outperforming the standard convolution-based HyDEA. 

• Generalizes seamlessly across diverse inner obstacle geometries in flow simulations without requiring network retraining. 2The rest of this work is organized as follows. Section 2 elucidates the numerical methodology, beginning with an introduction to the decouple immersed boundary projection method [51], followed by a comprehensive formulation of HyDEA. This section further describes the construction of the training dataset, the adaptation of MConv operator, the neural network architecture, and the overall training procedure. Section 3 systematically evaluates the performance of MConv-based HyDEA through multiple benchmark cases. 2.4. Neural Network architecture and model training 

In our previous work [44], we employed an unstacked DeepONet [17] to approximate the mapping from the input function space r to the output function space e. The branch network of the DeepONet employed the classic CNN-based U-Net [58] as its backbone to process rk discretized on uniform Cartesian grids. However, regarding specific flow problems, particularly those involving FSI, adopting non-uniform grid discretizations is crucial for balancing simulation accuracy and e ffi ciency. Consequently, extending the applicability of HyDEA to non-uniform grid discretizations represents a critical research objective. More specifically, within non-uniform Cartesian grids, the grid spacing exhibits spatial heterogeneity across the com-putational domain. Standard convolution operators, which rely on spatially shared kernel parameters, implicitly assume a uniform discretization. As a result, standard convolution operators are ill-suited for processing data on grids with spatially varying resolutions. This motivates the design of convolution operators that can adapt to variable grid spacings. To address the aforementioned limitations, Hu et al. [50] proposed an enhanced convolution operator, termed Mesh-Conv (MConv). By incorporating a local weight function based on the spatial relationships of neighboring nodes, MConv improves the adaptability of CNNs to process data on non-uniform structured grids. While the original formulation of MConv encodes both distance and angular information from eight surrounding nodes, the non-uniform Cartesian grid discretization addressed in this work maintains a strict orthogonal property. Consequently, the local weight calculation can be driven exclusively by the four directly adjacent source nodes, and the influence of the angle vector becomes redundant. Based on these considerations, we propose a simplified local weight: ¯ηp,q = exp (−Φ∆(∆p,q)2), (17) where ¯ ηp,q denotes the local weight at the target node, with ( p, q) representing its discrete spatial indices on the two-dimensional grid, as illustrated in Fig. 2. Φ∆, implemented as a neural network, is the weight function designed to encode the distance vector ∆p,q. Here, ∆p,q consists of the Euclidean distances between the target node ( p, q) and its four directly adjacent source nodes. By incorporating ¯ ηp,q, the input feature Xp,q is transformed into a weighted feature ¯ Xp,q:¯Xp,q = ¯ηp,q · Xp,q. (18) Subsequently, the weighted feature map ¯ X is processed by a standard convolution operator to yield the final output feature map Y. The overall computational workflow of MConv operator based on this simplified local weight is illustrated in Fig. 3. Regarding the handling of grid spacing information at the domain boundaries, we employ a nearest-neighbor padding strategy, wherein the missing grid spacing exterior to a boundary target node is defined to equal the grid spacing of its immediate internal neighbor. MConv is employed to replace a subset of the standard convolution operators within the U-Net architecture of the branch network described in [44] to enhance the model’s capability in solving the PPE discretized on non-uniform Cartesian grids. It is worth noting that the original MConv operator implementation in [50] was restricted to scenarios where the spatial dimensions of the feature maps remain constant. Initially, an average pooling operation is applied to compute the physical nodal coordinates corresponding to each of the K

levels of the U-Net. Based on these pooled coordinates, the level-specific distance vector maps are calculated. Specifically, for any given target node, we compute the absolute coordinate di ff erences between the target node and its four directly adjacent source nodes: the left and right neighbors along the x-axis, and the upper and lower neighbors along the y-axis. By computing these four directional distance components across all target nodes at a specific level K and arranging them spatially, we construct four distinct grid spacing maps ( ∆xK,left , ∆xK,right , ∆yK,up and ∆yK,down ). 2.5. Technical details 

Table 1 summarizes components, number of grid points, maximum-to-minimum grid spacing ratio ( ∆max /∆min ) and applications of HyDEA. Each implementation of HyDEA incorporates a CG-type method and DLSM. We employ the vanilla conjugate gradient (CG) method together with three preconditioned variants, namely, the incomplete Cholesky decomposition PCG (ICPCG) method, the Jacobi PCG (JPCG) method, and a 4-level Multigrid PCG (MGPCG-4) method as the CG-type method. For example: 

• HyDEA (ICPCG + DLSM-1-Conv) denotes the hybridization of the ICPCG method with the standard convolution-based DLSM for Case 1. 

• HyDEA (ICPCG + DLSM-2-MConv) denotes the hybridization of the ICPCG method with the Mesh-Conv-based DLSM for Case 2. DLSM and DeepONet are implemented utilizing Python and PyTorch . CG-type methods are sourced from the PETSc 

library [61], which is implemented in C, with its Python interface petsc4py .The Python -PetIBM interface is built using the Pybind11 library [62]. The DeepONet models are trained and deployed exclusively on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX4090, while remaining computations are executed on a single Intel Xeon Silver 4210R CPU. The source code for HyDEA is available on Github at https://github.com/HMB9666/HyDEA .

3. Results and discussions 

We systematically evaluate performance of the MConv-based HyDEA through three benchmark cases governed by viscous incompressible fluid dynamics, and provide comprehensive comparisons against both the standalone CG-type method and standard convolution-based HyDEA in Ref. [44]. Case 1: In Section 3.1, HyDEA is evaluated for two-dimensional (2D) lid-driven cavity flow with an embedded stationary circular cylinder. The computational grid is locally refined near the cylinder region, with ∆max /∆min ≈ 2.3. Case 2: Section 3.2 investigates more 2D flows past an obstacle. The computational grid is locally refined near the obstacle region, achieving ∆max /∆min ≈ 41. More specifically: 

• in Section 3.2.1, HyDEA is evaluated for classical 2D flow past a circular cylinder at Re = 100. 

• in Section 3.2.2, HyDEA is evaluated for 2D flow past an elliptical cylinder. 

• in Section 3.2.3, HyDEA is evaluated for 2D flow past the DARPA SUBOFF profile. 

• in Section 3.2.4, HyDEA is evaluated for 2D flow past an inline oscillating cylinder. Case 3: Section 3.3 evaluates the performance of HyDEA for 2D flapping elliptical wing at Re = 75. The computa-tional grid is locally refined near the elliptical wing region, achieving ∆max /∆min ≈ 28. For the training dataset preparation, we employ the parameter values m = 7000 and b = 0.6. These predetermined parameter values have been used in Ref. [44] and demonstrated excellent performance. 4. Conclusion 

In this work, we extend a hybrid framework that combines deep learning with classical iterative methods to accel-erate the iterative solution of the pressure Poisson equation (PPE) in incompressible flow simulations, from uniform to non-uniform Cartesian grid configurations. Recognizing that standard convolution operators are inherently ill-suited for processing data with spatially varying resolutions, we adopt the Mesh-Conv (MConv) operator that explicitly embeds grid spacing information directly into convolution operations. We design a multi-level distance vector map construction strat-egy to calculate distance vector maps across di ff erent hierarchical levels of the U-Net architecture, subsequently replacing a subset of standard convolution operators of the DeepONet’s branch network. Benchmark results underscore the superior generalizability and computational performance of the MConv-based Hy-DEA: 

• Reduces the number of iterations required to solve the PPE across diverse flows on non-uniform Cartesian grids, compared with standalone CG-type methods. 

• Outperforms the standard convolution-based HyDEA significantly on non-uniform Cartesian grid configurations where substantial variations in grid spacing exist across the domain. 

• Generalizes to diverse obstacle geometries in flow simulations with fixed neural network weights .22 -1.0

Although MConv-based HyDEA demonstrates excellent performance, its computational overhead and GPU memory requirements currently restrict the present study to 2D flow simulations. Furthermore, the existing network architecture is exclusively tailored for non-uniform Cartesian grid discretizations. Adapting HyDEA to arbitrary non-uniform grids would necessitate network architecture being capable of processing arbitrary non-uniform data, such as graph neural networks or Transformer; however, this would predictably introduce substantial computational overhead. Additionally, the current implementation of HyDEA requires retraining whenever a new set of grids is introduced, revealing limited generalizability across entirely arbitrary grid configurations. Consequently, future e ff orts will be directed toward refining the neural network architecture, optimizing training strategies, and enhancing code e ffi ciency. These improvements aim to facilitate large-scale three-dimensional flow simulations with HyDEA while simultaneously broadening its generalization capabilities across diverse grid topologies.
