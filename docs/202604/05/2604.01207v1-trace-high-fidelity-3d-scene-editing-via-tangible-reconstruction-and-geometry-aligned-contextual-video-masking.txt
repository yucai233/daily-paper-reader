Abstract. We present TRACE , a mesh-guided 3DGS editing frame-work that achieves automated, high-fidelity scene transformation. By anchoring video diffusion with explicit 3D geometry, TRACE uniquely enables fine-grained, part-level manipulation—such as local pose shift-ing or component replacement—while preserving the structural integrity of the central subject, a capability largely absent in existing editing methods. Our approach comprises three key stages: (1) Multi-view 3D-Anchor Synthesis, which leverages a sparse-view editor trained on our MV-TRACE dataset—the first multi-view consistent dataset dedicated to scene-coherent object addition and modification—to generate spatially consistent 3D-anchors; (2) Tangible Geometry Anchoring (TGA), which ensures precise spatial synchronization between inserted meshes and the 3DGS scene via two-phase registration; and (3) Contextual Video Mask-ing (CVM), which integrates 3D projections into an autoregressive video pipeline to achieve temporally stable, physically-grounded rendering. Ex-tensive experiments demonstrate that TRACE consistently outperforms existing methods especially in editing versatility and structural integrity.

# 1 Introduction 

Recent advances in 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) [42] have redefined high-fidelity real-time rendering, offering significant potential for applications ranging from interactive content creation to robotic simulation [60]. However, achieving high-fidelity 3DGS scene editing with diverse functionalities—spanning texture refinement to structural transformation—persists as a significant challenge. Existing 3D scene editing methods follow two primary paradigms based on their objectives. First, Appearance-centric Frameworks [13, 38, 43, 86, 114] focus on global or local style and color manipulation. These approaches an-chor 2D generative priors [8, 49, 51, 89] using the scene’s depth or epipolar con-straints, enforcing multi-view consistency [14,88,117] to propagate edits without compromising structural integrity. Second, Structural Modification Meth-ods [16, 53, 58, 87] handle complex tasks like object addition or removal. They typically introduce explicit geometric proxies ( e.g ., meshes, bounding boxes) or utilize SDS-based optimization [66, 79, 122] to guide the generation process.

In spite of this progress, high-fidelity shape editing in 3DGS scenes remains fundamentally challenging due to the following limitations: Geometric Insta-bility due to Weak 3D Grounding. Purely 2D-driven generative methods often fail to achieve structural changes, as they tend to prioritize superficial color and style edits over actual shape modification. Furthermore, without robust 3D priors to provide depth constraints, attempting 360-degree view synthesis from a single edited view invariably leads to severe ’back-side’ artifacts and a total loss TRACE 3

of structural integrity (see Fig. 2, left panel). Inflexibility and Misalignment of Explicit Geometry. Current 2D-to-3D lifting methods relying on iterative refinement suffer from significant computational overhead and slow performance. These methods typically converge toward "average" object shapes and require extensive manual tuning. Furthermore, the structural misalignment between gen-erative priors and 3D representations leads to poor scene integration, making them unsuitable for fine-grained local manipulation (see Fig. 2, right panel). These challenges suggests that a robust solution lies in the synergy between deterministic structural constraints and fluid generative refinement. First , we observe that while explicit 3D meshes provide the necessary spatial "scaffold-ing" to prevent geometric collapse, they often remain alien to the target scene’s lighting and texture distribution. Second , while video diffusion models excel at synthesizing temporally consistent textures, they lack the underlying 3D ground-ing to handle drastic topological shifts. Therefore , the key to high-fidelity 3DGS editing is not merely to "insert" new geometry, but to treat explicit meshes as geometric anchors that guide, rather than dictate, the generative process. By bridging this gap, the editing task evolves into a physically-grounded synthesis where the spatial certainty of 3D representations resolves viewpoint ambiguities, while the contextual adaptability of diffusion models ensures that new structures are natively harmonized within the original scene manifold. Based on this strategy, we introduce TRACE , a comprehensive 3DGS edit-ing framework composed of three key stages. To provide a spatially consistent reference for seamless alignment between the scene and new geometry, we first employ Multi-view 3D-Anchor Synthesis , which replaces spatially inaccu-rate and scale-ambiguous single-view cues with robust multi-view guidance. To facilitate this, we curated MV-TRACE—a multi-view editing dataset featuring 100k pairs of scene-coherent object additions and modifications. This allows our sparse-view editor to generate the reliable positioning anchors necessary for auto-mated, high-precision alignment. Following this, we perform Tangible Geome-try Anchoring (TGA) to synchronize the physical mesh with the scene’s coor-dinate system. Finally, TRACE employs Contextual Video Masking (CVM) 

to refine the visual output. By integrating the precise boundaries of 3D mesh projections with an autoregressive video generation pipeline, this stage ensures strict long-range geometry consistency in video inpainting, which ensures the final output is flicker-free and physically grounded.

In summary, our main contributions are as follows: 

– We propose a novel scene editing strategy that anchors the video diffusion process with tangible 3D priors. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that our framework achieves superior view consistency and semantic alignment, while uniquely enabling complex structural modifications with high efficiency.

# 2 Related Work 

2.1 Image and Video Editing 

Given the inherent scarcity of expansive 3D assets, current 3D editing frame-works heavily rely on the powerful generative priors of 2D models to facilitate high-fidelity modifications. Contemporary image editing methods range from training-free and efficient adaptation to compute-intensive frameworks. Training-free methods [1, 7, 17, 24, 48, 50, 59, 61, 64, 70, 85, 96–98, 100, 103, 109] offer a lightweight solution by utilizing techniques such as DDIM inversion [40, 77] and cross-attention manipulation [35]. These approaches facilitate zero-shot object-level and global modifications [112] by effectively re-purposing the attention maps of pre-trained models to align with target textual prompts. Conversely, compute-intensive frameworks achieve state-of-the-art control and superior fi-delity through dedicated optimization. This category includes training-based methods [2, 8, 37, 41, 78, 101, 104, 105, 111, 112, 115, 119, 120] that fine-tune gen-erative models on large-scale paired datasets, as well as recent advancements that scale up diffusion processes [10, 57, 83, 90] or adopt unified auto-regressive modeling [12,18,21,54,55,91,95] to establish more robust multimodal alignment. Building upon these image-level foundations, video editing has emerged as a promising avenue for 3D priors. While early research [5, 27, 67, 93] adapted T2I models via the integration of temporal layers, recent advancements such as MotionI2V [76] and VideoSwap [32] utilize dedicated diffusion backbones. These models rely on sophisticated motion-based regularization, such as optical flow or LoRA [36]-based optimization, to preserve temporal coherence. More recently, unified frameworks [6, 9, 22, 29–31, 45, 62, 65, 71, 72, 82, 118] like VACE [38] and AnyV2V [46] have introduced more efficient, training-free editing pipelines. TRACE 5

2.2 3D Scene Editing 

Recent 3D scene editing methodologies strive for multi-view consistency by bridging generative priors with 3D representations. Pioneering works like Instruct-NeRF2NeRF [34] and GaussianEditor [16] utilize Instruct-Pix2Pix [8] to itera-tively propagate 2D edits into 3D space. While GaussianEditor enhances pre-cision via region-of-interest extraction and GSEditPro [79] further improves lo-calization through cross-attention, these methods are often limited by the 2D editor’s inherent stochasticity [44, 106]. To enforce stronger constraints, person-alization approaches [15, 19, 23, 26, 28, 75, 99], such as TIP-Editor [122] and Per-sonalize your Gaussian [87], employ DreamBooth or LoRAs for content binding. However, they necessitate time-consuming per-scene fine-tuning. Alternatively, implicit alignment frameworks [121] like DGE [14] and Tinker [114] leverage epipolar geometry or video diffusion priors, with Tinker heavily relying on depth of the original scene. More recently, Native Gaussian Editing, exemplified by VF-Editor [68], directly predicts primitive variations but remains restricted to semantic shifts rather than complex structural reformations. Finally, geometry-guided anchoring methods [53] introduce explicit meshes for structural integrity. Meanwhile, a significant gap persists between current editing pipelines and recent breakthroughs in high-capacity Image-to-3D generation [3,11,73,81,110,113,116] and editing models. They remain largely underutilized in existing frameworks, despite offering immense potential. 

# 3 Method 

Following standard practices in 3D editing, TRACE builds upon an initial colmap reconstruction to perform 3D editing through three primary modules. First, we establish initial visual anchors via Multi-view 3D-Anchor Synthesis 

(Sec. 3.1), which leverages a 3D-LoRA fine-tuned model and VLM-guided prompt-ing. Then, we ensure structural accuracy through Tangible Geometry Align-ment (Sec. 3.2), where LRM-generated meshes are precisely aligned with the scene. Finally, the Contextual Video Masking module (Sec. 3.3) extends these edits to continuous trajectories using masked inpainting to maintain tem-poral coherence. The resulting frames are back-projected into a unified 3D Gaus-sian Splatting representation for final reconstruction.

3.2 Tangible Geometry Alignment (TGA) 

To bridge 2D edits with 3D space, we generate 3D priors using either mesh generators or editors. These priors are aligned with the original scene’s geometry using masks from multi views to determine the precise editing region. For existing objects, original point cloud are used; for newly added objects, sparse view feed-forward reconstruction results guide the localization (see Fig. 3 part b.). 8 J. Hu, Z. Zhang et al.

Task-Adaptive 3D Prior Generation. To accommodate diverse editing in-structions, we adopt a task-specific asset generation strategy. For complex scene edits, directly generating segmented parts ( e.g ., specific object components) of-ten introduces geometric redundancy and floaters that are difficult to remove later (see part b of Fig. 3, where the reconstructed wings contain redundant structures). We therefore employ mesh-based editors [3, 39], which provide bet-ter control over existing geometries. For simpler object insertion tasks, we instead use dedicated mesh generators [81] to create assets from scratch. In some cases, we first extract the primary subject from the generated mesh and perform edits on the target component to ensure cleaner geometric integration. 

Progressive Coordinate Registration and Alignment. The alignment of assets from diverse generators with the 3DGS scene is executed through the fol-lowing two-phase pipeline (see Fig. 5): Phase 1: Initial Pose Alignment. This initial phase establishes a consistent global orientation by aligning the generated asset with monocular depth priors. We first resolve the "up-vector" ambiguity by performing a cross-correlation analysis between multi-view asset renders and the reference scene. Once the principal axes are identified, we solve a similarity transformation problem to match the asset’s geometry with a monocular point cloud derived from the reference view. # 5 Conclusion 

We present TRACE , a novel and automated framework for high-fidelity 3DGS editing that effectively bridges the gap between 2D generative priors and 3D geometric consistency. The core of our approach lies in the integration of ex-plicit 3D masks as geometric anchors the video diffusion process. By leverag-ing our MV-TRACE dataset for robust anchoring, we introduce Tangible Geometry Alignment (TGA) and Contextual Video Masking (CVM) 

modules to transform explicit 3D meshes into geometric scaffolding for video dif-fusion. This strategy ensures synthesized assets are natively harmonized within the scene manifold, maintaining structural integrity across complex trajectories while uniquely enabling part-level manipulation . We believe TRACE es-tablishes a new paradigm where explicit guidance and generative flexibility work in synergy to produce seamless, structurally sound 3D reconstructions. TRACE 15
