## Abstract 

Dynamic 3D scene reconstruction is essential for immer-sive media such as VR, MR, and XR, yet remains challeng-ing for long multi-view sequences with large-scale motion. Existing dynamic Gaussian approaches are either Frame-Stream, offering scalability but poor temporal stability, or Clip, achieving local consistency at the cost of high memory and limited sequence length. We propose ClipGStream, a hybrid reconstruction framework that performs stream opti-mization at the clip level rather than the frame level. The se-quence is divided into short clips, where dynamic motion is

## 1. Introduction 

With the rapid advancement of immersive display tech-nologies such as virtual reality (VR), mixed reality (MR), 

> arXiv:2604.13746v1 [cs.CV] 15 Apr 2026

and extended reality (XR) [1–3], users increasingly demand high fidelity and interactive three dimensional media expe-riences. Compared with static scenes [4–11], producing dy-namic immersive content is much more difficult in both data acquisition and reconstruction algorithms. For data acquisition, synchronized multiview video cap-ture provides an effective solution for recording real world dynamics, offering rich spatial and temporal information that forms a strong basis for high quality reconstruction [12–17]. However, dynamic scene reconstruction [18] re-mains highly challenging. Unlike static environments [19– 22] that mainly involve reconstructing geometry [23–25] and appearance, dynamic scenes additionally require accu-rate modeling of temporal motion, which becomes difficult under large scale movements, complex interactions between humans and objects, and long sequence durations. Current dynamic reconstruction methods can be broadly categorized into two groups: Frame-Stream and Clip ap-proaches. Frame-Stream methods optimize the scene on a per-frame basis, enabling scalable reconstruction of ultra-long dynamic sequences. Dynamic3DGS [26] per-forms independent static reconstruction for each frame, and 3DGStream [27] models inter-frame motion through Neural Transformation Cache, yet such approaches often exhibit inter-frame jitter and accumulated reconstruction errors. Clip methods jointly optimize longer temporal clips (e.g., around 300 frames) using different spatio-temporal training strategy. 4DGS [28], 4DGaussian [29], and Space-TimeGS [30] effectively alleviate temporal inconsistency but incur substantial memory and computation costs, lim-iting scalability to long sequences. In addition, while both paradigms can handle subtle motion, they struggle to recon-struct dynamic scenes involving large or fast movements. In this paper, we propose ClipGStream, a hybrid multi-view dynamic reconstruction framework that integrates the advantages of Frame-Stream and Clip paradigms, while fur-ther enabling robust reconstruction of complex and large-scale motions through clip-level stream processing. The full video is divided into N short clips, each containing a continuous sequence of M multiview frames. The first clip 

Clip 0, serves as the Reference Clip, and the remaining clips 

Clip n∈[1 ,,N −1] , are treated as Source Clips. Our approach comprises two complementary strategies that align with intra- and inter-clip levels. (1) Intra-clip Training Strategy: The Reference Clip decodes static and dynamic features of anchors into Temporal Gaussians [31], followed by rasterization. For each Source Clip, we augment the anchor set with residual anchors on top of the Reference Clip’s anchors to capture newly introduced or displaced structures, and train an independent Spatio-Temporal Field ( ST F ) for that clip to model its local mo-tion. (2) Inter-clip Inheritance Strategy: To maintain temporal consistency across clips, each Source Clip inherits the anchors, static features, and decoder from the Reference Clip. All inherited components remain frozen during opti-mization, enabling stable reconstruction of arbitrarily long dynamic sequences. In summary, our contributions are as follows: • We propose the first Clip-Stream dynamic reconstruction framework, which simultaneously addresses large-scale motion and long-sequence modeling in multi-view dy-namic scenes. • We design a two stage training strategy. Intra-clip training employs residual initialization with independent ST F for robust modeling of complex and fast motions, while Inter-clip inheritance freezes shared components to maintain temporal consistency and enable stable reconstruction of arbitrarily long video sequences. • Extensive experiments on public benchmarks (N3DV, VRU) and newly released Long 360 dataset demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance. 

## 2. Related Work 

Dynamic scene reconstruction can be categorized into 

monocular and multiview settings. The monocular setting aims to reconstruct dynamic scenes from video sequences captured by a single moving camera [32–38], but its recon-struction quality is often limited by restricted viewpoints and insufficient geometric constraints. In contrast, the mul-tiview setting leverages multiple synchronized cameras to capture rich spatial and temporal information, making it more suitable for producing high-quality dynamic content for commercial applications such as VR, AR, and XR. Therefore, this work focuses on multiview dynamic scene reconstruction, which has recently been actively explored in [27, 30, 39–42]. Current dynamic scene reconstruction methods fall into two main paradigms: Frame-Stream and 

Clip methods. 

Frame-Stream methods train dynamic multiview videos in a frame-wise manner. StreamRF [40] adopts Plenoxels [43] for scene representation, enabling fast on-the-fly training. Dynamic3DGS [26] extends this idea by optimizing simplified 3D Gaussian structures for high-quality novel view synthesis. 3DGStream [27] introduces a Neural Transformation Cache (NTC) to maintain tempo-ral continuity between frames, while later methods such as iFVC [44] and Hicom [45] further enhance storage ef-ficiency and reconstruction speed. These approaches are highly scalable and can process ultra-long dynamic se-quences, yet they often suffer from inter-frame jitter and accumulated errors over time. 

Clip methods formulate dynamic scene reconstruction as a joint optimization problem over all frames, aiming to model coherent spatio-temporal structures within a unified representation. K-Planes [46] and HexPlanes [47] represent scene dynamics through learnable multi-plane features. D-rendering

NeRF [48], Nerfies [35], 4D Gaussian [29], DeformableGS [34], Swift4D [39], and SC-GS [49] model per-frame mo-tion using continuous deformation fields to capture non-rigid dynamics in complex scenes [50, 51]. 4DGS [28] further adopts an explicit 4D representation of geometry and appearance, while SpaceTimeGS [30], Gaussian-Flow [52], and NSFF [53] estimate continuous motion trajec-tories to model long-range temporal dynamics. Although these methods achieve temporally stable and high-quality reconstructions, their joint optimization over all frames pre-vents them from scaling to long dynamic sequences. As a result, training is typically performed on short clips, and extending them to longer sequences often leads to temporal discontinuities across clip boundaries. To enable high-quality reconstruction of long-sequence dynamic scenes with complex motions, we propose a novel 

Clip-Stream framework, ClipGStream . Within each clip, a clip training strategy is employed to maintain tempo-ral consistency and ensure stable reconstruction, while across clips, a stream-based scheme enables continuous and scalable learning. By integrating the advantages of both paradigms, ClipGStream unifies clip and stream modeling within a single coherent framework, achieving robust per-formance on ultra-long sequences and large-scale dynamic motions. 

## 3. Method 

In this section, we present our method, ClipGStream. We first review the preliminary concepts of 3DGS and Scaf-foldGS in Sec. 3.1. In Sec. 3.2, we describe how our Clip-Stream framework combines the strengths of both Frame-Stream and Clip approaches, achieving scalable and temporally consistent dynamic scene reconstruction. Next, Sec. 3.2.1 introduces the Intra-clip Training Strategy , in which the Reference Clip and each Source Clip are mod-eled using independent Spatio-Temporal Fields ( ST F ), and large motions in Source Clip are compensated via residual anchors. Following that, Sec. 3.2.2 presents the Inter-clip Inheritance Strategy , which shares the decoder and fea-tures of anchors across Clips to enforce temporal consis-tency. Finally, the overall loss is provided in Sec. 3.3.

3.2. ClipGStream 

To address the limitations of Frame-Stream and Clip paradigms, we propose a unified Clip-Stream framework, ClipGStream, that divides a dynamic sequence into tempo-rally coherent clips. The sequence begins with a Reference Clip , which is fully optimized to establish a stable spatio-temporal representation, followed by multiple Source Clips 

that are trained based on it. This design combines the stabil-ity of clip-based optimization with the scalability of stream-based training, forming the foundation for our intra-clip and inter-clip strategies introduced below.

3.2.1. Intra-clip Training Strategy 

In the Reference Clip training stage, as shown in Fig. 2, we formulate dynamic scene modeling as decoding both static and dynamic features for each anchor [31]. The decou-pling of static and dynamic features enables us to inherit the static information in subsequent Source Clips training, thereby ensuring temporal consistency (Fig. 3). Specifically, the anchor set A0 is initialized from the COLMAP [57, 58] point cloud aggregated over all frames within Clip 0. Unlike ScaffoldGS [56], our anchors contain only a 3D position μ ∈ R3 and two feature vectors: a static feature fs ∈ R64 and a dynamic feature fd ∈ R64 , where the latter is obtained from a spatio-temporal field ( ST F ). The ST F 0 is implemented as a 4D hash grid h0 followed by a fully fused MLP ϕ0. Given an anchor μ0 at time t, the dynamic feature is computed as 

fd, 0 = ϕ0(h0(μ0, t )) . (4) We then concatenate fd, 0 with its static counterpart fs, 0 and feed them into the decoder d(·) to generate the Temporal Gaussians 

Gt, 0 = d([ fs, 0; fd, 0]) , (5) which are subsequently rasterized and supervised by ground-truth images. This process jointly optimizes the an-chor positions μ0, the static features fs, 0, and the parame-ters of ST F 0. However, directly extending this process to subsequent Source Clip is challenging in dynamic scenes with large-scale motion. To maintain temporal consistency across clips, we first inherit A0 when training the Source Clip (as detailed in Sec. 3.2.2). Anchors inherited from the Reference Clip often undergo substantial spatial displace-ments that cannot be accurately captured through deforma-tion alone. To handle such cases, we introduce a residual-based anchor compensation strategy , which augments the input anchor set with newly emerging or displaced anchors, thereby improving spatial coverage and motion adaptabil-ity. Specifically, we construct the anchor set An for Source Clip based on the residual between the COLMAP-derived anchors Acn of Clip n and the base anchors A0 from Refer-ence Clip:

3.2.2. Inter-clip Inheritance Strategy 

For the Reference Clip, the training jointly optimizes the an-chors A0, their static features fs, 0, the spatio-temporal field 

ST F 0, and the decoder d that maps feature representations to Temporal Gaussian attributes. However, naively independently optimizing each subse-quent Source Clip ( Clips n∈[1 ,N −1] ) would require reinitial-izing these components, leading to inconsistent scene rep-resentations across clips. As shown in Fig. 5, such inde-pendent optimization results in severe flickering in static regions and degraded rendering quality in dynamic areas (Fig. 8 (a)). ## 4. Experiment 

4.1. Implementation 

For our method, all MLPs consist of two layers with ReLU, followed by Sigmoid or normalization at the output. Both dynamic and static feature dimensions are set to 64. Our method use the Adam optimizer [61] with the learn-ing rate schedule of 3DGS [54] within each clips. The learning rate scheduler is reinitialized at the start of each clip’s training, rather than inheriting the state from the Ref-erence Clip. Reinitializing the scheduler prevents the learn-ing rate from becoming too small as training progresses, which would otherwise hinder effective optimization.

4.2. Datasets 

Long 360. We primarily evaluate our method on the Long 360 dataset [31, 62]. It consists of 1,400 frames recorded at 4K resolution from 36 cameras uniformly arranged in a 360-degree circular setup, capturing a dynamic basketball game. This provides a challenging benchmark for reconstruction under high-speed motion. We use cameras 0, 10, 20, and 30 as test views, and the remaining 32 for training, with all images downsampled by a factor of 2. 

N3DV [14]. N3DV is a widely adopted benchmark, cap-tured with a 21-camera multi-view system at a resolution of 2704×2028 and 30 FPS. It is a standard dataset for multi-view dynamic reconstruction with fine-scale motion. The train/test settings follow the prior works [27, 38, 40]. 

VRU [39]. The VRU dataset is captured with a 34-camera multi-view system, recording real-world basketball games at 1920×1080 resolution and 25 FPS. We follow the same experimental settings as Swift4D [39]. Compared to N3DV, it features larger motion ranges and serves to evaluate ro-bustness in real-world conditions.

Ablation on M and N . Given a total of N input frames, the video is divided into segments of M frames each. A criti-cal limitation of prior methods is their inherent requirement for M = N , meaning the entire video sequence must be processed at once. Directly extending it to multiple clips by partitioning frames (i.e. M < N ) results in noticeable tem-poral inconsistencies (Fig. 9, LocalDyGS 140 Clips video). Our method addresses this by enforcing consistency across clips, enabling flicker-free handling when M < N .

## 5. Conclusion 

We present ClipGStream , a unified training framework that bridges Frame-Stream and Clip dynamic Gaussian paradigms for high-quality modeling of long sequences and large-scale motion. Specifically, we uniformly divide long dynamic video sequences into multiple clips. Then, we pro-pose a Intra-clip Training Strategy to effectively capture clip-specific dynamic features. To mitigate flickering arti-facts across-clips, we further introduce a Inter-clip Inher-itance Strategy that enforces temporal consistency across clips. Extensive experiments on Long 360, VRU, and N3DV demonstrate that our framework achieves state-of-the-art performance in complex motion scenes and general-izes effectively to sequences of arbitrary length. 

Limitation. Our approach depends on camera poses esti-mated by COLMAP. In challenging cases with low image overlap or large textureless areas, COLMAP may yield in-accurate calibrations, which can affect reconstruction qual-ity. Future work will focus on integrating more robust pose estimation to further improve generalization. 6. Acknowledgments
